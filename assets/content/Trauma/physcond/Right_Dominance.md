Right Dominance is the natural favoring of the right side of the body — not the hand alone, but the whole side working in concert: the right hand grips and strikes, the right arm bears and throws, the right leg leads and balances, the right eye aims. It is the common condition of most folk, so unremarkable that it is rarely noticed until injury or training forces the off-side into service. A person with Right Dominance stands in orthodox fighting stance, reaches instinctively with the right, and plants the right foot forward when set to act.

### What Shapes It

Dominance appears partly innate — an infant shows a side-preference before any training begins — and partly reinforced by culture, which in most lands favors and drills the right. Deliberate practice can narrow the gap between the sides, and injury to the dominant side can force a lifetime of awkward relearning, but the underlying preference usually endures. A rare few train or are born to no dominance at all — they are **ambidextrous**, and carry neither this characteristic nor Left Dominance.

### Narrative Uses

Right Dominance is the assumed baseline in most encounters: tools, weapons, saddles, and doorways are built for it, and an orthodox opponent reads as familiar. Its significance surfaces mostly by contrast — when a right-dominant fighter is disarmed or wounded in the right arm and must fight clumsily left-handed, or when they face a rare left-dominant opponent whose unfamiliar angle unsettles them.
