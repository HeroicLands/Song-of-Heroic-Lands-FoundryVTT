Responsiveness is the habit of reacting swiftly and favorably to the needs of others or changes in circumstance. In the practical world of smithcraft, soldiering, and governance, a responsive person proves invaluable—quick to sense when aid is needed and quicker still to act. This is not mere haste; it is attentiveness coupled with willingness to bend toward the needs of the moment.

### Quirk

At its most wholesome, responsiveness appears as a practical vigilance. A responsive yeoman notices when a neighbor's fence has begun to sag and mends it before rain comes. A responsive steward hears of a sudden grain shortage in the village larder and quietly arranges for additional stores from neighboring farmsteads. There is no fanfare, no expectation of reward—only the natural reflex to align one's actions to what is needed.

**Example:** A village leader who, upon hearing of an unexpected flood sweeping down the river valley, immediately organizes the evacuation of inhabitants to higher ground, coordinates the distribution of what food and blankets can be spared, and posts watches to warn of further danger.

### Impulse

When responsiveness becomes excessive, a person moves toward compulsive agreeability. They cannot say no when asked for help, no matter how unreasonable the request or how depleted they are. Such a person begins to spread themselves too thin, taking on obligations that pull them from their own work and family. They grow restless when they perceive unmet needs around them, fidgeting and anxious until every minor problem has been addressed—regardless of whether it was their burden to carry.

**Example:** A castle steward who, having bent so often to his lord's whims and the requests of servants, finds himself unable to refuse any demand, working himself to exhaustion and neglecting his own household's welfare.

### Disorder

At its most pathological, responsiveness warps into manic obligation. The person becomes enslaved to the perceived needs of others, prioritizing the comfort of strangers over their own survival. They may ruin themselves financially by lending coin they do not have, or compromise their own health and safety to appease demands that were never legitimate. In extreme cases, they begin to hallucinate new needs in others—problems that do not actually exist—driving themselves into frantic activity born of anxiety rather than genuine necessity.

### Physical Symptoms

Fidgeting, restlessness, an inability to sit still. The body appears perpetually tense, muscles knotted. Such people often develop tremors from constant hurry and eat irregularly, forgetting to take meals in the midst of their compulsive action.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Constant loops of self-recrimination. "Have I done enough? Have I missed someone in need? What if someone suffers because I rested?" The mind races with imagined scenarios of failure and catastrophe.

### Emotional Distress

Anxiety, guilt, and a crushing sense of inadequacy. Even while helping others, the person feels they should be doing more. Sleep is fitful; rest brings no peace.

**Example:** A shrine-keeper who, having responded so completely to every pilgrim's plea for counsel and comfort, finds himself waking in the night seized by panic, convinced that someone is in peril and abandoned, driving him to leave his post to search for distressed travelers who do not exist.
