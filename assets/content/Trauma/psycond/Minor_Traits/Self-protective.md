Self-protectiveness is the habit of guarding oneself—one's goods, one's feelings, one's vulnerability—against harm. A self-protective person does not offer trust freely; they have learned, through experience or temperament, that the world contains threat. They build walls. They keep coins hidden. They reveal little of their inner heart until they have thoroughly tested the character of another.

### Quirk

At its most reasonable, self-protectiveness is practical caution. A traveler who avoids well-known bandit roads is self-protective. A woman who does not display her jewels in the market is self-protective. A man who listens more than he speaks until he has taken the full measure of a stranger is engaging in self-protection of the sensible sort. Such people remain open to genuine connection; they simply take their time, observe carefully, and do not expose their vulnerabilities to those who have not earned the privilege.

**Example:** A traveling merchant who is always wary of bandits and thieves, concealing his wealth by dressing plainly, varying his routes to avoid predictability, and taking care never to reveal the full extent of his goods to potential rivals—ensuring the safety of both himself and his valuable cargo.

### Impulse

When self-protectiveness becomes compulsive, it hardens into emotional rigidity. The person begins to see threat everywhere, even where none exists. They cannot accept gifts without suspicion that the giver intends manipulation. They cannot form close friendships without testing, repeatedly, whether the friend will betray them. They create distance even with those who have proven loyal, unable to rest in trust. This constant vigilance exhausts them and isolates them; those around them grow weary of the endless questioning and begin to withdraw, confirming the self-protective person's belief that connection is impossible.

**Example:** A noble whose repeated checking that his servants are not stealing from him, whose constant testing of his wife's faithfulness through small deceits and traps, gradually destroys his household until even those who were genuinely loyal begin to resent him and draw away.

### Disorder

At its most pathological, self-protectiveness becomes paranoia. The person cannot rest for belief that they are surrounded by enemies. They may hide their assets so thoroughly they themselves forget where they have placed them. They may refuse necessary help when ill or injured, believing that physicians or healers mean to harm them. Some become hostile, lashing out preemptively at anyone who approaches them. Others retreat into complete isolation, convinced that the only safety lies in severing all human connection.

### Physical Symptoms

The body is perpetually tense, muscles locked in readiness for threat. Sleep is broken and shallow, interrupted by nightmares of betrayal and attack. The person is often found holding things close—coins clutched in a fist, arms crossed protectively over the chest. Hands are frequently fisted; the jaw is clenched.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Loops of suspicion and recrimination: "Are they trustworthy? What does this gesture truly mean? What are they hiding from me?" A constant inner voice that interprets neutral actions as hostile and assigns hidden motives to innocent remarks.

### Emotional Distress

Loneliness coupled with inability to be alone. Persistent fear and a sense that true safety is impossible. Shame at the inability to trust others, masked by blame directed outward ("Everyone is dishonest").

**Example:** A lord who has become so convinced that even his closest advisors mean to rob him that he hides his wealth in secret locations, tests his household repeatedly with false accusations and contrived betrayals, and finally, in his certainty of universal deceit, executes a loyal steward on baseless suspicion, destroying what remained of his household and dying alone, his hidden treasures eventually claimed by those who truly did intend his ruin.
