Sociability is the natural draw toward others, the ease of mind that comes in company and conversation. A sociable person finds energy in gatherings, derives pleasure from exchange, and moves comfortably among strangers. They remember names, sense when someone needs a kind word, and can talk at length on a dozen topics. In the tight-knit world of village and town, where news and trade flow through face-to-face meeting, the sociable person becomes a vital thread in the community's fabric.

### Benign

At its best, sociability manifests as genuine warmth and interest in others. A sociable merchant remembers his regular customers' children by name and asks after their wellbeing; a sociable soldier becomes the heart of camp morale. Such folk are excellent news-carriers, not from mischief but from a natural interest in people's lives. They make friends readily, settle disputes through conversation, and thrive in taverns and market-squares. Their company is sought because they make others feel noticed and valued.

**Example:** A tavern owner who knows all the villagers by name and enjoys engaging in lively conversation with patrons, recalling details of their lives, their trades, their troubles. His establishment becomes a popular gathering place for news, gossip, and community bonding—not because the ale is exceptional, but because he makes every guest feel welcome.

### Impulse

When sociability turns compulsive, a person can no longer bear solitude. They will seek out company even when it is poorly timed or unwelcome—interrupting work, lingering after conversation has grown stale, or following acquaintances about town. They begin to need constant validation through interaction, becoming restless and anxious when alone. They may talk excessively, dominating conversations, or offer unsolicited advice and opinions. Others begin to edge away, sensing something needy beneath the chattiness.

**Example:** A cloth merchant's wife who has taken to visiting neighbors at all hours, sitting uninvited at their hearths, and discussing their private matters with other housewives at the market. Her energy exhausts those around her; she seems unable to read when she is no longer wanted, and her absence from a gathering wounds her deeply.

### Disorder

At its extreme, the need for company becomes tyrannical. The person cannot endure even brief solitude—they grow frantic, believing themselves forsaken or dying. They may make unreasonable demands on family and friends' time, accuse people of abandonment over minor delays, or seek out any companion regardless of character or harm. Their clinginess repels others, deepening their isolation and desperation. Some resort to disruption or melodrama to regain attention.

### Physical Symptoms

A sociable person shifts easily in their seat, gestures openly, and smiles readily. At impulse levels, they fidget when alone, their leg bouncing, their fingers restless. In disorder, separation anxiety manifests as a tightness in the throat, shallow breathing, and an almost frantic physical restlessness.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Why am I alone? Has someone done me wrong? Will they come back? At disorder level, the thoughts spiral: _I am unwanted. Everyone abandons me. I cannot survive this emptiness._

### Emotional Distress

Benign sociability brings contentment and belonging. Impulse-level creates an undercurrent of anxiety and neediness that even the person recognizes as unseemly. Disorder manifests as acute loneliness even in company, desperation, and cycles of rage and self-pity when connection fails.

**Example:** A scrivener's apprentice named Thomas, once cheerful and well-liked, has become so dependent on constant company that he trails behind the journeymen, interrupting their work with chatter. When they close the workshop door to focus on a commission, he falls into despair, convinced he has been cast out. He has begun leaving the city for days at a time seeking different company, neglecting his training and making himself unreliable.
