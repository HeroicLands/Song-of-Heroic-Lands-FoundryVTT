Team-orientation is the natural bent toward cooperation and collective endeavor. A team-oriented person does not hoard glory or compete for status at the expense of the group. They see the strength in combined effort and take genuine pleasure in the success of those around them. In a world where a village survives harvest only through shared labor, such people are the sinew that binds community together.

### Benign

At its best, team-orientation is a gift to any collective enterprise. A team-oriented guild member shares his techniques freely with younger craftsmen, knowing that the quality of the guild's work—not his individual reputation—is what sustains them all. A team-oriented soldier watches not only for his own safety but for that of his shield-mates. A team-oriented steward delegates duties well, trusts her subordinates, and celebrates their successes as her own. Such people are the backbone of stable institutions.

**Example:** A member of a medieval guild who works closely with fellow craftspeople, sharing tools and knowledge, celebrating the prosperity of the whole guild over individual accolades, ensuring that both the quality of their collective work and the welfare of every member reflects well on all of them.

### Impulse

When team-orientation becomes excessive, the person loses the ability to maintain healthy boundaries. They begin to subsume their own identity into the group, unable to voice disagreement or assert their own needs for fear of disrupting group harmony. They take on far more than their share of work to keep the team functioning, eventually becoming resentful. They struggle to make any decision without consulting the group, paralyzed by the belief that individual choice is inherently selfish. In extreme cases, they may enable poor behavior in group members rather than face conflict, protecting those who exploit them in the name of team unity.

**Example:** A shipwright so committed to his company that he covers for a fellow worker's laziness, does not report theft by a crew member, and takes on double labor to maintain the appearance of smooth functioning—ultimately compromising the quality of their work and drawing suspicion upon his own integrity.

### Disorder

At its most destructive, team-orientation becomes a complete dissolution of self. The person loses all individual will and purpose. They may engage in harmful acts at the group's behest, even acts that violate their own conscience. They cannot function outside the group, falling into deep despair if separated. Some begin to believe they are telepathically connected to their group, that the group's thoughts are their thoughts. In the worst cases, they may refuse care or assistance that would benefit them personally, choosing to suffer so that the group need not sacrifice.

### Physical Symptoms

Signs of overwork and exhaustion: darkened circles beneath the eyes, visible loss of weight, tremor in the hands from constant labor. The person often appears disheveled, having neglected personal care in favor of group needs.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Constant loops of self-doubt: "Have I done enough? Does the group value me? Will they reject me if I disagree?" A desperate, repeating need for external validation of belonging.

### Emotional Distress

Deep anxiety about rejection and abandonment. Resentment that builds silently, masked by forced cheerfulness. Despair at the impossibility of meeting an ever-expanding set of group demands.

**Example:** A galley-hand whose need for belonging has led him to perform tasks both foolish and criminal at the captain's word, compromising his own health and moral sense, until he becomes so dependent on the crew's approval that he cannot conceive of a life apart from them—and when the ship sinks and the crew is scattered, he takes his own life, unable to imagine purpose or belonging in isolation.
