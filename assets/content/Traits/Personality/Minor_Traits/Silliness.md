Silliness is a bent toward frivolity and play, an inability or unwillingness to take matters seriously when gravity is demanded. A silly person finds humor in nearly everything, even circumstances that warrant solemn consideration. They jest when counsel is needed, prank when trust is necessary, and treat weighty affairs as opportunities for mirth. While lightness of spirit is valued in feast and court entertainment, silliness in moments of genuine peril or dignity can isolate a person from respect and trust.

### Benign

At its gentlest, silliness is simple good humor and wit. A person so inclined enjoys a jest, plays harmless pranks, and makes others smile with a ready quip. They are often the welcome jester at feast, the companion whose company lifts spirits during dull labor. They can read a room well enough to know when levity is fitting, and their silliness serves a social purpose—it eases tension, builds camaraderie, and makes work less bitter. Others value their presence for the lightness they bring.

**Example:** A young page who is known for his clever tongue and lighthearted nature. He mimics the pomposity of elder knights in ways that make even grave warriors crack a smile, and he is often called upon to entertain guests or chase melancholy from the hall. His pranks—chalk upon a chair, a frog in a boot—are gentle and much laughed upon.

### Impulse

When silliness becomes compulsive, the person can no longer moderate their levity. They jape when a lord is speaking on matters of state, they laugh at funerals, they interrupt solemn oaths with quips. Their behavior becomes tiresome to those around them, and worse, it begins to seem disrespectful or unstable. Serious folk grow weary; they begin to exclude the silly person from matters of importance, viewing them as unreliable or childish. The person feels the shift in how they are treated but cannot seem to stop themselves—the impulse to jest, to mock, to play is too strong.

**Example:** A young page who is always making jokes, pulling pranks, and laughing at inappropriate times—during religious services, when the lord is angry, when a servant has suffered injury. The knights, though sometimes amused, have begun to regard him with frustration and disappointment. They see him as lacking the seriousness required for his future duties, and they have begun to give him fewer responsibilities, treating him as a liability rather than an apprentice.

### Disorder

At its most pathological, silliness becomes a compulsive deflection from reality. The person cannot face anything difficult, painful, or tragic without dissolving into inappropriate laughter or mockery. They may have witnessed genuine trauma and respond with jests; they may mock the dying or the grieving. Their behavior becomes deeply offensive and isolating. Some believe them mad; others regard them as callous or morally defective. They often sense the revulsion they inspire but are trapped in their own need to perform, to deflect, to never let earnestness show.

### Physical Symptoms

A silly person is often restless—fidgeting, laughing readily, their body loose and animated. At impulse levels, they may grimace or contort their face to make others laugh, or their laughter may become high-pitched and uncontrollable. In disorder, the laughter can become almost manic, their movements jerky and desperate, sweat beading their brow as they perform.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Make a joke. Make them laugh. Do not let anyone see you are serious or hurting. At disorder level: _If I stop joking, they will see what I really am. I must never be still. Never be silent._

### Emotional Distress

Benign silliness often masks shyness or sensitivity. At impulse levels, the person begins to sense they are not being taken seriously and feel a growing loneliness beneath their jests. In disorder, they experience profound alienation—they perform constantly to keep others engaged, yet no real connection forms. Exhaustion, despair, and a creeping sense of being utterly alone accompany the incessant laughter.

**Example:** A young court musician named Cedric, once merely cheerful, has descended into a compulsive, almost frantic jocularity. He cannot attend a funeral without making quips, and when a fellow musician fell from a horse, Cedric made jest of it even as the man lay in agony. The court has begun to shun him, and some whisper he is touched by demons. Cedric senses the rejection but finds he cannot stop—the moment silence falls, a dreadful panic rises in his chest, and he must perform again, must make them laugh, or he fears he will dissolve into something far darker.
