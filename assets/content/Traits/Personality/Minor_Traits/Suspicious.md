Suspicion is a wariness toward others' intentions and a tendency to doubt their sincerity or honesty. A suspicious person questions motives, watches for deception, and approaches new relationships and situations with caution. In a world where betrayal can cost one's livelihood or life, some measure of wariness is wisdom; yet when suspicion becomes entrenched, it poisons all connection and peace.

### Benign
At its most practical, suspicion is simply careful discernment. A suspicious person is unlikely to be cheated in a trade, for they examine goods closely and question prices. They do not reveal secrets to unreliable folk and do not trust their valuables to untested hands. They are good judges of character, having developed an ear for evasion and an eye for inconsistency. In positions of authority—a bailiff, a gatekeeper, a purser—this trait makes them effective guardians. They are rarely surprised or made a fool of, and they protect those dependent upon them through watchful caution.

**Example:** A blacksmith's apprentice who, after hearing rumors of theft in the village, begins to closely watch anyone who enters the workshop. He carefully monitors customers and fellow apprentices, ensuring that no tools or materials go missing. Though his suspicions keep the workshop secure, those around him feel his watchfulness as neither oppressive nor unfounded—he is protecting honest work.

### Impulse
When suspicion becomes habitual, a person starts to perceive duplicity where none exists. They notice small inconsistencies in others' stories and interpret them as signs of deception. They withhold trust even from those who have proven reliable, constantly alert for the betrayal they are convinced will come. They may test others' loyalty with tricks, or interrogate them about minor details, searching for the lie they are sure is hidden. They become exhausting to be near; others sense the constant scrutiny and begin to avoid them or grow resentful. Their suspicion often becomes self-fulfilling—people withdraw, which the suspicious person interprets as confirmation of their untrustworthiness.

**Example:** A craftsman who has grown so suspicious of theft that he has begun to accuse his own workers of petty crimes. He counts tools obsessively, watches his apprentices during their work, and questions why they spend time together, fearing they are plotting. His constant accusations and vigilance have made the workshop atmosphere unbearable; workers are leaving, and those who remain are resentful and defensive.

### Disorder
At its extreme, suspicion collapses into a kind of paranoia in which nearly everyone is believed to be plotting against the person. They interpret ordinary events as evidence of conspiracy—a casual glance becomes a hostile stare, a missed greeting becomes a deliberate slight, a change in tone suggests nascent betrayal. They may refuse to eat food prepared by others, convinced of poisoning. They become socially isolated because they cannot maintain relationships based on trust. Some grow hostile, preemptively attacking those they believe are about to betray them. Others withdraw entirely, retreating into fortified homes or wilderness, unable to bear the burden of perceived threat.

### Physical Symptoms
A suspicious person often has an alert, watchful posture—eyes frequently moving, shoulders braced. At impulse levels, they may appear tense and restless, unable to relax. In disorder, the physical strain becomes pronounced: jaw clenched, brow furrowed, sometimes a hollow-eyed exhaustion from constant vigilance and interrupted sleep.

### Intrusive Thoughts
What are they hiding? Why did they say that? Are they laughing at me? At disorder level: *Everyone is against me. There is no one I can trust. They are all waiting to strike.*

### Emotional Distress
Benign suspicion brings a sense of security born from clear-eyed assessment. Impulse level introduces constant low-grade anxiety and an undertone of hurt—the person feels surrounded by dishonesty and cannot understand why no one seems truly honest. In disorder, the emotional toll is catastrophic—profound isolation, fear bordering on panic, and often a deep sense of betrayal and unworthiness that manifests as rage or despair.

**Example:** A miller's wife named Eleanor, once cautious but reasonable, has descended into consuming suspicion. She believes her husband is unfaithful despite no evidence; she suspects her servants are stealing despite careful accounting; she interprets her neighbors' kindnesses as mockery. She has begun to refuse visitors and keeps her daughter confined, convinced the girl will be corrupted. She checks the locks on doors multiple times each night and hides valuables in dangerous places, then forgets where they are. Her family despairs; Eleanor is trapped in a prison of her own making, convinced that safety lies only in complete isolation and control.
