Watchfulness is the habit of attending closely to one's surroundings, noticing particulars that slip past the drowsy or inattentive. A watchful person takes in sights, sounds, and small movements; they register a loose board, a tell-tale scuff in dust, the tilt of a head that betrays deception. In a world where threats come from forest and sea, from brigand and wild beast, this attentiveness can mean the difference between a full belly and starvation, between safe passage and ambush.

### Benign

Watchfulness at its everyday best is simple alertness—the practice of keeping one's eyes and ears open. A watchful merchant notes the quality of fleece before purchase; a watchful fisherman reads the water's surface for signs of his catch. It requires no paranoia or constant strain, merely a habit of noticing. Such folk make reliable sentries, steady hunters, and cautious travelers. They rarely miss a hand reaching for the purse-strings or a stranger's approach along the road.

**Example:** A hunter in the forest who keenly observes the movements of animals and the subtle signs of their presence—broken twigs, the spoor of deer, the alarm-calls of wood-pigeons—allowing him to track and capture game with great skill and patience.

### Impulse

When watchfulness becomes compulsive, the mind cannot rest. A person in this state finds themselves constantly scanning, always suspecting they've missed something vital. They may check locks repeatedly, peer over their shoulder at regular intervals, or catch themselves staring at strangers' faces, searching for lies. The attentiveness that once served them now exhausts them; their jaw clenches, their breathing quickens. They struggle to enjoy a meal or follow a conversation because their attention keeps snagging on peripheral details.

**Example:** A garrison captain who, even during feast-day celebrations, cannot relax his vigil. He counts the torches to ensure none has been snuffed, watches the servants pouring wine, and positions himself where he can observe all exits—his hypervigilance making those around him nervous and uncomfortable.

### Disorder

At its most severe, watchfulness collapses into obsessive surveillance of nearly everything. The person becomes convinced that catastrophe will strike if they stop watching even for a moment. They may refuse to sleep, sitting at a window or doorway all night. They track the movements of family members, questioning them about their whereabouts. They notice threats that do not exist—a fleeting shadow becomes a prowler, a casual glance becomes a hostile stare. Other folk begin to avoid them, unsettled by the intensity of their scrutiny.

### Physical Symptoms

A watchful person's body speaks plainly: the eyes are sharp and frequently moving, the shoulders often braced as if against surprise. In the impulse and disorder states, the jaw tightens, the brow furrows, and sleep becomes shallow and fitful. The sufferer may develop headaches from sustained focus.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Where is the threat? What have I failed to see? The mind circles endlessly, replaying moments, searching for overlooked details. In disorder, the thoughts become fragmentary and repetitive: _Look. Watch. Check again. Did I miss it?_

### Emotional Distress

At benign intensity, watchfulness brings a quiet confidence. At impulse level, it brings anxiety and a creeping sense that something is wrong, though nothing concrete can be named. In disorder, it becomes a prison—exhaustion, despair, and the fear that one's vigilance is the only thing keeping calamity at bay.

**Example:** A miller named Godwin, once a careful observer of his mill's workings, has grown so watchful that he no longer trusts his assistants. He stands at the wheel all hours, watching the grain flow, convinced that any moment of inattention will result in disaster. His eyes are hollow from sleeplessness, his hands shake, and he has accused honest men of theft because he thought he saw them lingering near the stores. His family fears for his mind.
