Reticence is the virtue of restraint in speech—the habit of measuring words before they leave the tongue. A reticent person does not scatter opinions like seed corn or seek the ear of every listener. Instead, they speak thoughtfully, sparingly, and only when they judge their words will serve some purpose. In a world of gossip and court intrigue, such quiet is often mistaken for wisdom.

### Benign

At its healthiest, reticence is contemplative silence. A reticent person listens before they speak, observes before they judge. A reticent scholar spends nights with her books before she offers counsel; a reticent knight listens to the grievances of three petitioners before declaring his judgment. Their silence has weight. When they do speak, people lean in to hear.

**Example:** A monk who spends his days in quiet prayer and study, speaking only when necessary and then with great deliberation, his rare words often containing wisdom gathered from years of silent reflection.

### Impulse

When reticence hardens into compulsion, it becomes evasiveness and withdrawal. The reticent person becomes unable to speak freely even when clarity would heal conflict. They answer questions with evasions, hide their true thoughts behind platitudes, and create rifts in their relationships through their refusal to communicate. What began as thoughtful silence becomes a wall—and those around them grow frustrated and suspicious of the silence they cannot penetrate.

**Example:** A merchant whose refusal to speak plainly with his wife about financial troubles causes her to imagine far worse problems than truly exist, leading to misunderstanding and deepening distance between them.

### Disorder

At its most pathological, reticence becomes a prison. The person becomes nearly mute, unable to voice even basic needs or feelings. They may withdraw from all social contact, communicating only in whispers or brief, cryptic phrases. This isolation can breed paranoia—the belief that others are speaking ill of them, that speech itself is dangerous. Some such people may harbor violent thoughts they dare not voice, letting resentment fester in silence until it erupts without warning.

### Physical Symptoms

A tightness in the throat and jaw. Difficulty meeting another's eyes. The body grows still and tense when conversation draws near. Speech, when it comes, may be halting or accompanied by stammering and long, uncomfortable pauses.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Constant self-scrutiny: "If I say this, will they judge me? Will my words be misused? What if I say the wrong thing?" A voice of self-doubt that prevents even simple utterances from leaving the mouth.

### Emotional Distress

Loneliness and a sense of profound disconnection from others. Frustration at the gap between what one wishes to express and what one can actually voice. Shame about the wall one has built, coupled with inability to dismantle it.

**Example:** A scholar who has withdrawn so completely into silence that when an injustice befalls him—an accusation of theft by a rival—he cannot summon the words to defend himself, allowing false accusations to stand and his reputation to be destroyed by his own enforced muteness.
