Sensitivity is an openness to feeling—one's own emotions and those of others register acutely, with little filtering. A sensitive person resonates with the moods and sorrows of those around them; they weep readily, feel joy sharply, and are moved by beauty or cruelty in equal measure. In a harsh, demanding world, this trait can be a gift—it builds compassion and insight—but it can also become a burden, making one's path through life fraught with hurt and exhaustion.

### Benign

At its most positive, sensitivity manifests as genuine compassion and artistry. A sensitive person is attentive to others' suffering and moved to relieve it. They make loyal friends, caring kin, and excellent healers or counselors. They may pursue arts—music, poetry, painting—channeling their acute feeling into beauty. Their emotional depth makes them wise beyond their years in some ways, though they may seem fragile in others. People seek their counsel because they are listened to fully and without judgment.

**Example:** A troubadour who, deeply moved by the emotions of those around him, composes and performs songs that capture the joys and sorrows of his audience with such fidelity that listeners often weep or find laughter shaking their frames. His art heals something in the community; he is cherished for his ability to name feelings that others cannot voice.

### Impulse

When sensitivity becomes excessive, the person begins to absorb everyone's emotions as if they were their own. A day in the market—with beggars, arguing merchants, mothers scolding children—leaves them devastated and unable to function. They cry easily, often without knowing why; the sight of a caged bird or mistreated animal may send them into prolonged grief. They become unable to hear criticism without falling into despair. They withdraw from company to protect themselves, spending long hours alone in an attempt to regulate their overwhelmed feelings. Others begin to see them as fragile, weak, or self-indulgent.

**Example:** A merchant's daughter whose sensitivity has become almost unbearable. She hears of a servant's ailing mother and grieves for days, unable to manage her household duties. She witnesses a beggar's hunger and becomes ill herself, unable to eat. Her family has begun to regard her as a liability—unable to attend feasts, unable to manage a visit to market, weeping at trifles. They worry she will never be fit for marriage.

### Disorder

At its most severe, sensitivity becomes a complete vulnerability to the world. The person cannot engage with normal life; they are devastated by ordinary hardships. A harsh word haunts them for weeks; a stranger's sorrow becomes their own trauma. They may refuse to leave their home, eat only what is prepared with great care, and speak so rarely that some believe them mute. They are often thought to be ill or cursed. The accumulation of empathic pain becomes so immense that suicide may seem the only respite.

### Physical Symptoms

Sensitive folk often have a pallid or delicate appearance. Their eyes are bright, almost tearful. They may flinch at loud noises or sudden movements. In impulse states, their hands may shake, their breathing becomes shallow, and they may develop frequent headaches or stomach complaints. In disorder, the physical effects compound—weight loss, insomnia, or conversely, a near-catatonic lethargy.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Why does the world hurt so much? How can I bear this pain? At disorder levels: _I am too broken for this world. Everyone would be better if I were not here._

### Emotional Distress

Benign sensitivity brings depth and compassion. Impulse level introduces constant anxiety, dread of exposure to others' pain, and shame about one's own "weakness." In disorder, the emotional pain is relentless—the person feels they are drowning in everyone's suffering simultaneously and cannot save themselves.

**Example:** A monastery's novice brother named Edmund, once known for his gentle care of the sick, has become so sensitive to suffering that he can no longer tend the ill. The sight of a fevered child in agony sends him into profound distress; he hears the patient's pain as if it were his own flesh burning. He has begun to avoid the infirmary entirely, wracked with guilt at his abandonment. His confusion and despair have grown so intense that the abbot has begun to question whether Edmund is truly called to the monastic life, or whether his affliction will consume him.
