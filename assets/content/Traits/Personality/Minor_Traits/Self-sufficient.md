Self-sufficiency is the capacity and inclination to provide for oneself without constant reliance upon others. A self-sufficient person masters the practical skills necessary to survive—growing food, mending tools, raising livestock, fashioning shelter. They trust their own judgment, act independently, and take responsibility for their circumstances without casting blame elsewhere. In a medieval world where interdependence is the norm, the self-sufficient person stands apart, valued for their reliability but sometimes regarded with suspicion for their isolation.

### Benign
At its best, self-sufficiency is a sturdy independence rooted in genuine competence and wisdom. A self-sufficient person is prepared, capable, and relatively unburdened by need. They may live modestly but comfortably, their smallholding productive, their craft profitable. They are reliable—one can depend upon them to keep their word and manage their obligations. They are often sought as partners in ventures because they do not require constant oversight. They take reasonable pride in their achievements and do not seek undue praise or sympathy.

**Example:** A homesteader who lives on a small plot of land, growing his own food, raising livestock, and making his own clothes and tools. He relies on no one but himself and his immediate family to survive. When neighbors require aid, he offers practical help—a gift of seed, knowledge of timber-working, or a day's labor—but he does not cultivate dependency or seek gratitude.

### Impulse
When self-sufficiency becomes rigid, a person begins to refuse help even when it would be wise to accept it. They nurse wounds rather than call a physician, undertake repairs beyond their skill, and exhaust themselves rather than ask a neighbor for assistance. They grow suspicious of those who offer aid, interpreting kindness as interference or pity. They become isolated by their refusal to be vulnerable or interdependent. They may present themselves as scornfully superior to those who need help, judgmental of what they perceive as weakness in others. Their isolation begins to show its cost—they appear worn, their work suffers, their relationships grow thin.

**Example:** A craftsman whose skill is considerable but whose refusal to delegate or seek counsel has begun to damage his reputation and his health. When his apprentice suggests a method that might ease the work, he dismisses it with disdain. When his wife suggests hiring help during a large commission, he regards her as unfaithful to his self-reliance. He drives himself to exhaustion and produces inferior work, all while insisting that to accept help would be to admit defeat.

### Disorder
At its most pathological, self-sufficiency becomes a kind of isolation so profound that it resembles pathological pride or paranoia. The person cannot accept help without violent rejection; they interpret offers of assistance as accusations of failure. They grow hostile if anyone suggests they are overextended. They may sabotage relationships intentionally, pushing away family and allies to "prove" they need no one. Some starve in the midst of plenty rather than beg; others die of treatable wounds rather than admit to need. The compulsion to need no one becomes indistinguishable from self-destruction.

### Physical Symptoms
A self-sufficient person carries themselves with straightness and deliberation. They are often hardened by labor, with callused hands and weathered skin. At impulse levels, they may appear gaunt from overwork, their eyes sharp with strain. In disorder, they may show signs of neglect—unkempt appearance, signs of untreated injury, a hollowness suggesting insufficient food or rest.

### Intrusive Thoughts
I must manage alone. To ask is to fail. At disorder level: *If I depend on anyone, they will betray me. I am only safe if I need no one.*

### Emotional Distress
Benign self-sufficiency brings contentment and quiet pride. Impulse level introduces a grinding fatigue and a creeping resentment of others for their perceived weakness. In disorder, the emotional state becomes one of profound alienation—the person pushes everyone away, yet feels desperately lonely. Bitterness and a sense of being fundamentally unlovable often accompany the most severe cases.

**Example:** An elder craftsman named Aldwin, once admired for his independence, has become so rigid in his self-reliance that he refuses all aid. When his eyesight began to fail, he refused to see a physician, convinced his body would heal itself. When his hands grew arthritic, he refused assistance with heavy lifts, damaging expensive commissions and his own joints further. His children have offered to help support him; he has cast them off as ungrateful and weak. He now lives alone in increasing squalor, producing inferior work, yet refuses all offers of aid. His former apprentices speak of him with sad pity rather than the respect he once commanded.
