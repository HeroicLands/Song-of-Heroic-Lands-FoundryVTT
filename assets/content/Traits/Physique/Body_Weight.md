Body Weight is the simple measure of a person's mass — how much flesh and bone and blood they carry in the frame God gave them. It shapes everything from how far they can travel before tiring, to whether they can slip through a narrow window, to whether their war-horse strains under them. A plowman heavy with muscle and fat for winter survival reads very differently from a Sinalë courier thin as a rail from a life of swift travel through mountain passes.

### What Shapes It

Weight derives most obviously from a person's physical frame — height, bone density, muscular development, and the season's store of fat. A laborer in the fields develops thick shoulders and a weighty middle; a ascetic friar subsisting on bread and water remains slight. Age plays a role: youths are often leaner, while settled merchants and wives put on flesh from comfort and good living. Labor itself shapes it — a smith or mason will weigh more muscle than a scribe. Ancestry matters too: Khazári tend toward solidity even when not muscular; many Sinalë remain light-boned regardless of their strength. Diet and prosperity over years consolidate the weight; famine strips it away. A soldier on campaign will lighten measurably over weeks of short rations.

### Interpretation

A slight adult human, perhaps 100-130 pounds, might be a youth, a scholar unused to labor, or simply someone whose frame is naturally lean. A typical working adult ranges 140-200 pounds — mason, reeve, soldier, merchant's wife. The heavier end of normal, 200-240 pounds, suggests either genuine muscular development or a well-fed life of ease. At extremes, a person might weigh less than 80 pounds (starving, diseased, or simply very young) or exceed 280 pounds (exceptional muscle, or else obesity from idle plenty). Such extremes mark a person visibly: the skeletal hermit, the broad barrel-chested champion.

### Narrative Uses

Weight matters when climbing rope, traversing collapsing floorboards, or trying to hide. A heavy-set knight in full armor will sink in boggy ground where a slight scout travels safely. A portly merchant becomes a natural target for jest and judgment. Conversely, visible weight — the shoulders of a stonemason, the belly of a prosperous burgher — can mark status and capability. In a season of famine, every character's weight becomes precarious: a person of middle weight will visibly hollow, their strength waning. Healing from wound or sickness is measured partly in whether a person regains their natural weight. In genealogy and inheritance disputes, weight and build are sometimes mentioned as evidence of legitimacy: 'She has her father's broad shoulders.
