Favored Parts describes which side of the body — hand, foot, eye — a person naturally favors through habit and constitution. Most folk favor the right hand, some the left; a rare few have trained themselves to ambidextrous capability. Equally, a person might be dominant in their right leg for balance, or favor the left shoulder when bearing weight. These preferences shape fighting stance, how they grip a tool, which eye they aim with.

### What Shapes It

Handedness appears to be partly innate — an infant shows preference for one hand before training begins — and partly learned. Cultural training reinforces the dominant hand: in some societies, left-handed children are discouraged and even forced to switch, creating awkwardness that lasts a lifetime. A person forced to wield sword left-handed after their right arm was crippled must retrain muscle and instinct. Accident and injury reshape favor: a craftsman who breaks their dominant hand might develop genuine ambidexterity by necessity. Deliberate training, especially from youth, can create a warrior or dancer who favors either side nearly equally, though rarely with true mastery in both.

### Interpretation

A right-favored person is the common case — they naturally grip, throw, and strike with the right side. In combat, they stand in orthodox stance. A left-favored person is less common and sometimes viewed with suspicion in superstitious communities; in battle, they present an unfamiliar angle that orthodox fighters must adjust to. An ambidextrous person — one who has trained to use either hand with near-equal skill — is rare and often marks a warrior of unusual dedication or a craftsperson of exceptional talent. Partial ambidexterity is more common: a soldier might wield a sword primarily right-handed but throw projectiles left, or defend equally well from either side.

### Narrative Uses

Favored parts create texture in characterization. A scar on a person's dominant hand hints at a past wound and partial recovery. A character forced to fight left-handed for the first time in life — after injury or disarming — reveals their true skill level without the advantage of habit. In dance and sport, favored parts matter: a rightward-leaning dancer moves differently from a leftward one. In dueling, meeting a left-favored opponent for the first time unsettles orthodox fighters. Over time, a character with a severed dominant hand might retrain entirely to their off-side, marking the ordeal visibly in their new fighting style.
