Syndactyly is a condition of the flesh wherein two or more digits are bound together, their bones and skin fused as if by a hand other than nature's own. This webbing may be partial, binding only the flesh betwixt the digits while the bones remain distinct, or complete, fusing bone with bone such that the digits move as one. To the superstitious, a webbed hand is a mark of aquatic ancestry, a sign of blessing by water-spirits, or a curse of divine punishment—interpretations that vary wildly from realm to realm.

### Benign

At its mildest, syndactyly affects function only marginally. The fused digits retain most of their mobility, and the person learns early in childhood to adapt. A webbed hand may prove advantageous in swimming or handling rope. Such people live without shame, their condition noted but unremarked, and they pursue their callings much as any other soul—smith, farmer, priest, or scribe—suffering no true disability.

**Example:** A child born with webbed fingers, whose village, knowing the old tale of the water-keepers of the northern lakes, regard her with a mixture of reverence and curiosity, seeing in her condition not a curse but a link to powers beneath the waves.

### Impulse

As the fusion tightens or affects more digits, function becomes noticeably impaired. Writing becomes laborious, fine craftwork—the threading of a needle, the tying of a knot—grows frustratingly difficult. The person must learn compensatory techniques: holding tools differently, adopting grips that would seem awkward to others. Social embarrassment begins to seep in. Children may point or whisper. The person finds themselves performing extra feats of dexterity to prove their worth, driving themselves to exhaustion to compensate for the limitation they cannot hide.

**Example:** A youth whose webbing extends across three fingers, making the work of a wright or carpenter possible but slow, pushing himself to complete tasks faster than is safe, injuring himself repeatedly in the attempt to prove he is no less capable than his unblemished peers.

### Disorder

When the fusion is severe—when most or all of a hand is bound together into a single, club-like appendage—the hand becomes nearly useless for precision work. Such a person cannot feed themselves with cutlery, cannot dress themselves, cannot manage the basic tasks that grant dignity. They become dependent on others for acts they once took for granted. The psychological weight is crushing: their own flesh has betrayed them, and in many villages they are shunned as bearers of curse-mark, condemned to lives of beggary or servitude.

### Physical Symptoms

The webbed flesh is often thickened and scarred from years of strain. Movement is visibly restricted; the person may compensate by favoring the other hand, leading to unequal development of musculature. In severe cases, the fused digits may become swollen and tender, prone to infection where moisture gathers between the bound skin.

### Intrusive Thoughts

Constant awareness of the stares of others. Shame over the mark, fear that others believe one to be cursed. Compulsive checking—is the webbing spreading? Is it worsening? Will the other hand be seized by the same fate?

### Emotional Distress

Profound isolation born of visible difference. Resentment toward one's own flesh. Despair at the limits imposed by a condition one did not choose and cannot escape. Many such people retreat inward, avoiding company and public places where their hand might be remarked.

**Example:** A scribe whose complete syndactyly has rendered both hands nearly useless for fine writing, who now earns his bread as a beggar outside the cathedral, listening to others speak of the life he was once training for, his worth diminished in the eyes of all around him by a mark of flesh he did not deserve.
