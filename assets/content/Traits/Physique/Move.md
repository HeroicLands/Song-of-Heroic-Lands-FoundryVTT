Move represents the base running or striding speed at which a person travels — how quickly their legs carry them over short distances in battle or chase, or how many leagues they cover in a day's journey on foot. It is the fundamental tempo of their motion, shaped by physiology but also by habit, conditioning, and will.

### What Shapes It

Move derives primarily from Agility and Strength — a person with nimble feet and strong legs moves faster than one heavy or ungainly. Size matters greatly: a Khazár of stocky build will have a different natural pace than a Sinalë courtier, even if both are equally capable. Age affects it; youths in their prime move faster than elders or the very young. Conditioning is crucial: a soldier drilled daily in formation will maintain a faster march than a scribe. Injury or disease can diminish move for months; recovery restores it gradually. A person laden with heavy gear moves slower than one unencumbered. Terrain shapes short-term move as well: a warrior sprints differently across open ground than through forest or rubble.

### Interpretation

A slow mover — aged, injured, or simply ungainly — might cover 15-18 feet in a combat round, or 15-20 leagues in a day of walking. An average person maintains 20-24 feet per round in battle, or 20-24 leagues overland with rest. The swift — a young soldier in training, a hunter seasoned in pursuit — might move 26-30 feet per round, covering 25-30 leagues daily. The exceptionally fast, rare in common folk, exceed 30 feet and can travel 30+ leagues if driven, though such pace cannot be sustained without rest. These differences compound: over a pursuit of hours, the swifter pursuer closes the gap steadily, while the slower fugitive tires.

### Narrative Uses

Move becomes critical in ambush and rout. The captain who can outmarch his enemies reaches the ford first and chooses the battlefield. A messenger with high Move delivers word ahead of the next day's riding; one slow is overtaken. A character pursuing a fleeing thief across rooftops and through alleys must match or exceed their quarry's Move to close the distance. Encumbrance — a knight in full plate, a merchant's wife in trade-good-laden furs — slows even the naturally swift. Over long journeys, high Move reduces time and supplies needed; low Move stretches a month's march to six weeks. Fatigue matters: even the fleetest of foot slows when exhausted. In stories of flight and chase, Move becomes a narrative element — the long-legged knight catching the small brigand, the youthful maiden outdistancing her pursuers through a crowded market.
