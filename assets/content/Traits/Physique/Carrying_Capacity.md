Carrying Capacity describes the total weight a person can bear without undue strain — how many provisions they might pack for a long march, how much spoil they can carry from a raid, whether they can lift and drag a wounded comrade to safety. It is a direct expression of Strength, Size, and Endurance working in concert.

### What Shapes It

Strength is the primary shaper: a muscular laborer or blacksmith can carry far more than a scribe. Endurance contributes — the person with hardy lungs and strong heart bears weight longer without faltering. Size plays a subtle role: a larger frame can distribute load across broader shoulders and a deeper chest. Age and conditioning matter too: a youth of good health carries more than an elderly person of equal strength would. Injury diminishes capacity: a man with a wrenched shoulder cannot carry a full sack even if his strength seems undiminished. Fatigue erodes it sharply — after a hard day's march, the same person who carried 120 pounds fresh-legged cannot manage 80 without collapsing.

### Interpretation

At the lower end, a person might carry comfortably 40-60 pounds — a small backpack, bedroll, tools, and rations. This suits scribes, merchants, and folk not accustomed to labor. A typical laborer or common soldier manages 80-120 pounds: a full kit with armor, weapons, and provisions. A strong smith, mason, or seasoned soldier carries 140-180 pounds — a sack of grain, a full suit of plate and mail, plus a weapon, plus personal kit. Exceptional strength pushes this to 200+ pounds, though carrying such a load over miles requires breaks. A person in extreme straits might push beyond their natural capacity briefly — adrenaline and desperation grant temporary advantage — but the cost is paid in collapse or injury.

### Narrative Uses

Capacity shapes practical adventure. A party laden with spoil from a manor-house — chests of silver, bolts of silk — must decide who carries what, and whether a hired porter becomes necessary. A character pursuing enemies across difficult terrain must choose between speed and the weight of armor. In siege or hardship, a character with high capacity becomes invaluable: they can haul water, carry wounded, transport materials that others cannot. Conversely, weakness in carrying capacity creates dependency and narrative tension. A noble reduced to carrying their own gear for the first time discovers unexpected strength or unexpected fragility. A raiding party must leave behind plunder if their best bearer is injured.
