A medium-sized workbook, roughly eight inches by ten, bound between thin oak boards faced in tanned leather and closed with a single bronze clasp. Some thirty sheets of parchment are sewn into signatures within, enough for months of field notes, rough sketches, specimen pressings, ledger entries, or draft correspondence. Scribes, sages, herbalists, physicians, and mercantylers all carry its like—a workaday book meant for the road, the hedge, and the counting-stall rather than the library shelf.

The travelling book is built for use, not for ages. Parchment warps with damp, the boards scuff and split, and the spine eventually gives. A careful owner rebinds it every few years or retires it to an archive once its working pages are full. The value listed reflects only the materials and the binder's labor; a book filled with the owner's own notes, recipes, accounts, or observations is worth many times more to the one who wrote it, and potentially a small fortune to a rival or successor who knows what is recorded inside.

For a village herbalist, a circuit physician, or a junior clerk, the price of such a book represents many months of a craftsman's earnings. They are saved toward for years, gifted upon the completion of an apprenticeship, commissioned in thanks for some great service, or passed down from a master to a favored student. A working professional who has finally bought a fresh travelling book often treats it as a mark of their station as much as a tool of their trade, and its loss is reckoned in grief as well as in coin.
