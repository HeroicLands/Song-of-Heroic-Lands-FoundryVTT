Frostbite is the local freezing of skin and underlying tissue, most often at the body's extremities — fingers, toes, ears, nose, and cheeks — where circulation is poorest. Unlike hypothermia it is a localized injury rather than a systemic one, but lost tissue does not regrow and severe cases end in amputation.

### Symptoms

Affected areas first prickle and burn, then go numb. The skin turns waxy pale or grey-white, feels hard or rubbery to the touch, and may form clear or blood-filled blisters on rewarming. Deep frostbite blackens the tissue irreversibly.

### Progression

Superficial frostbite ("frostnip") recovers fully if rewarmed promptly. Deep frostbite causes lasting damage: nerves do not fully heal, the affected part remains cold-sensitive for life, and severely frozen flesh dies and sloughs away or must be cut off to prevent gangrene.

### Treatment

- Do not rewarm in the field if there is any chance of refreezing — refreezing destroys the tissue
- Once in shelter, immerse the affected part in warm (not hot) water until pliable
- Do not rub the area or break blisters
- Pad and protect the limb; treat any blackened tissue as a wound at risk of infection
