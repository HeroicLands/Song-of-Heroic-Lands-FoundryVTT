Eloquence is the art of clear and persuasive speech—the capacity to choose the right words, frame them persuasively, and deliver them with confidence. It shapes how effectively a person can convince, inspire, teach, or deceive through language. An eloquent speaker is heard; an inarticulate one is often ignored even when speaking truth. Eloquence is not mere prettiness of language but the power of words to accomplish intention.

### Incoherent

Incoherent speakers struggle to put thoughts into words. What seems clear in their mind comes out jumbled, contradictory, or impossible to follow. They grope for words, start sentences and abandon them, and leave listeners bewildered about what they meant. Others lose patience with them quickly, and their arguments fail not from being unsound but from being incomprehensible.

### Muddled

Muddled speakers manage basic communication but lack clarity and force. Their words tumble out in no particular order, they repeat themselves without seeming to notice, and their points are obscured rather than illuminated. They can be understood if listeners are patient, but they are rarely persuasive and often seem foolish.

### Average

Average folk speak clearly enough for ordinary purposes. Their meaning is plain, their words adequate, and listeners understand what they intend. They are neither articulate nor inarticulate, and most communication happens without difficulty. Most people speak in this ordinary fashion.

### Articulate

Articulate speakers choose words with care and speak with a natural assurance. Their meaning is always clear, and there is a pleasantness to listening to them. They explain ideas effectively, teach well, and can present an argument such that listeners understand every point. They are sought out for counsel and believed readily.

### Expressive

Expressive speakers seem to paint with words. They have a natural gift for the right phrasing, the apt metaphor, the telling detail. Listening to them is a pleasure; their arguments seem irresistible, and their teachings linger in the memory. They can move audiences to tears or laughter, inspire crusades, or persuade the unwilling. They are rare gifts in any community.

### In Practice

When a merchant must convince a lord to grant trading rights, the incoherent one leaves the lord confused and irritated, the petition denied. The muddled speaker makes points but confuses the lord with contradictions, and the lord grants nothing. The average merchant presents adequately and receives a measured decision. The articulate merchant presents so clearly that the lord understands every advantage and grants provisional rights. The expressive merchant leaves the lord so convinced of mutual benefit that the lord offers partnership and funding besides.
