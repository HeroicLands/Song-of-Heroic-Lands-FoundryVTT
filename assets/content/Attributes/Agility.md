Agility is the quickness and fluidity of bodily movement—the capacity to move swiftly, to dodge, and to maintain balance and control. It differs from dexterity, which concerns hand-and-eye coordination, and from strength, which measures power. Agility is what allows a person to move through a crowded marketplace without stumbling, to survive a fall from a horse, and to strike quickly and smoothly.

### Blundering

Blundering individuals move without grace or coordination of the larger body. They stumble over level ground, cannot catch themselves as they fall, and collide with obstacles. Their larger movements seem chaotic, as though their limbs betray their intentions. They are clumsy at climbing, hesitant at horseback, and generally look ill at ease in their own skin.

### Clumsy

Clumsy folk move with less grace than ideal. They bump into things, recover awkwardly from stumbles, and move with visible effort. A clumsy person looks uncomfortable when asked to move quickly, and their larger gestures lack economy. They are not dangerous to themselves, but nor do they move with confidence.

### Average

Average folk move adequately. They walk without stumbling, fall without serious damage, and possess normal reflexive stability. Most people are agile in this ordinary way—present in their bodies, capable of the movements daily life requires, but nothing more.

### Nimble

Nimble individuals move with evident grace and control. They are quick and sure-footed, rarely stumble, and recover swiftly if they do. A nimble person leaps stairs two at a time, climbs easily, and dances well. They move through crowded rooms without apparent effort or consciousness.

### Graceful

Graceful folk possess movement that is almost beautiful to behold. Every gesture is economical, every step flowing seamlessly into the next. A graceful warrior moves as though dancing, and a graceful dancer seems to float. Even mundane movements—rising from a chair, turning to answer—have an almost artistic quality.

### In Practice

When a rope breaks beneath a person climbing a cliff, the blundering climber falls helplessly. The clumsy one makes a desperate grab and tumbles onto a ledge below. The average climber falls far but lands on stone somehow and survives maimed. The nimble climber catches themselves with a hand and stabilises on a hold. The graceful climber somehow twists mid-fall and lands running, barely bruised, as though they had planned for this eventuality.
