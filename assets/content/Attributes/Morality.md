Morality measures the depth and consistency of a character's ethical conscience—their capacity to distinguish right from wrong, and their willingness to act according to that discernment. It is the principle that governs choices in moments of temptation, ambition, or hardship. A person's moral fiber shapes not only individual decisions but also their reputation, the trust others place in them, and ultimately their place in community and faith alike.

### Amoral

The amoral person has no internal ethical compass whatsoever. Concepts of right and wrong are abstractions to them, useful only insofar as they avoid punishment. Their choices arise purely from appetite, fear, or the desire for advantage. Such individuals are rare, and those who know them instinctively trust them with nothing of value.

### Immoral

The immoral character knowingly chooses what they understand to be wrong, and does so with intent. They may delight in cruelty, actively deceive, or take what is not theirs—not out of ignorance, but out of deliberate malice or indulgence. The corrupt courtier, the faithless merchant, the blackmailer—all know the good they reject.

### Hypocritical

Hypocrites profess virtue while harboring vices in private. They follow the outward forms of morality—paying lip service to honor, charity, or devotion—while their secret deeds contradict their words. Their conscience troubles them only when discovered, and they are swift to justify themselves by pointing to the failings of others.

### Unscrupulous

The unscrupulous person cuts corners and bends rules with little remorse. They care moderately for right and wrong, but care far more for their own advancement or comfort. They will not murder for coin, but they will overcharge, lie on oath, or betray confidence if the risk seems small enough.

### Conscientious

Conscientious folk genuinely seek to do right. They feel the weight of conscience keenly and are troubled by wrongdoing—both their own and others'. They make an earnest effort to keep their oaths, deal honestly, and live according to faith or code, though they may stumble through weakness or ignorance.

### Principled

The principled person lives according to a clear and unwavering code. Their commitments are rooted in conviction, and they will endure hardship rather than compromise those commitments. They are trusted implicitly because their course is predictable and their word is inviolable.

### Virtuous

The virtuous go beyond mere principle. Their ethical life is woven so thoroughly into their character that right action flows from them almost naturally. They seek the good not grudgingly but with grace, and they inspire others by example.

### Paragon

Paragons of virtue are legends made flesh—individuals whose moral conviction and steadfast goodness reshape the world around them. Their very presence seems to call forth the better nature in those near them, and their sacrifices for principle become the stuff of tales.

### In Practice

Consider a merchant faced with the chance to adulterate wine and pocket the difference. The amoral merchant sees only profit. The immoral one delights in the deception. The hypocrite might do it secretly while ostentatiously funding a chapel. The unscrupulous one might dilute just slightly, rationalizing that the buyer will not notice. The conscientious merchant feels genuine conflict but ultimately refuses, accepting the loss. The principled merchant refuses absolutely and makes a show of it. The virtuous merchant refuses and helps the buyer find good wine at a fair price elsewhere. The paragon's generosity becomes known throughout the shire, and a rival competitor, shamed, begins keeping honest measure as well.
